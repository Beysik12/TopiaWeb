Will Robot take my Job: The Age of AI

Industrial robots are mechanical arms that may be programmed to move a tool through a series of actions, such as a paint sprayer or a welding torch. Computer-controlled steel mills that can be run by a small number of personnel are another example of automation. Since the 1970s, there have been increasingly more of these robots employed. Approximately 2 million industrial robots are currently employed globally, primarily on assembly lines for automobiles and electronics, and each one replaces one or more human workers. Another reason economists like Bessen don't see AI generating mass unemployment anytime soon is the technology's limits. He states, in agreement with many others, "Automation is nearly always about automating a task, not the complete job." There are also very few jobs that are entirely routine, despite the fact that every employment involves at least a few regular tasks that may use AI. In fact, according to Bessen, of the 50,000 employment recorded in the 1950 census, "there was only one occupation that you could argue was definitively automated out of existence - elevator operators," and today there are practically none.
